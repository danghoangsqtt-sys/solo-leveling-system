Urban Farming Initiatives

Reading Passage 1

You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 1-13, which are based on Reading Passage 1 below.

In recent years, the concept of urban farming has evolved from a niche environmental movement into a crucial component of future city planning. As global populations continue to urbanize at an unprecedented rate, traditional agricultural supply chains are increasingly strained. Urban agriculture—ranging from rooftop hydroponic greenhouses to vertical indoor farms—offers a potential solution to food security challenges while simultaneously reducing the carbon footprint associated with transporting produce over long distances.

"The integration of agricultural systems into urban architecture is not merely about food production; it's about redefining the metabolic processes of our cities."

Proponents argue that vertical farming, in particular, maximizes yield per square meter by utilizing controlled-environment agriculture (CEA) technology. These systems control temperature, light, and humidity to optimize plant growth, often resulting in crop yields that are significantly higher than those of traditional field farming. Furthermore, hydroponic and aeroponic systems use up to 90% less water than conventional soil-based agriculture, making them particularly attractive in regions facing water scarcity.

Fig 1. Controlled-Environment Agriculture (CEA) vertical setup.

However, critics point out the substantial initial capital required to establish these high-tech facilities, as well as the intensive energy consumption necessary to power the artificial lighting and climate control systems. While renewable energy sources can mitigate the environmental impact, the economic viability of urban farming on a scale large enough to feed a city remains a subject of intense debate among agronomists and urban planners.
